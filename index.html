The term "Bushido" translates to "the way of the warrior", reflecting the samurai's commitment to martial arts and honour. Bushido's principles were adopted for ethical training across Japanese society, and the elements of the Bushido code persist in Japanese martial arts.

史

The term Bushido was first used in the 16th century, though its concepts emerged during the Kamakura period (1192-1333), the Kamakura period saw the establishment of Japan's first military government, with the shogun as the hereditary leader. Bushido was influenced by Zen Buddhism and Confucian thought, with martial spirit as a core ideal.
The legacy of bushido remains significant in Japan, often regarded as a code of honour and ethical conduct in modern Japanese society. Bushido is sometimes romanticised, particularly in literature and media. Bushido continues to influence modern Japanese culture and society, affecting business practices, communication, and martial arts; the samurai spirit is reflected in corporate behaviour, emphasising trust, respect, and harmony in business relations. Modern interpretations of bushido are used to enhance communication skills and personal development in contemporary society. In martial arts, bushido principles guide practitioners in ethical behaviour, self-discipline, and personal growth beyond just physical combat.

The seven virtues were developed to enhance self-discipline and performance, these principles have evolved into the samurai code and are integral to Japanese culture. Modern individuals can use these virtues as a guiding light in their daily lives, the virtues promote clarity, power, and vitality applicable to all life aspects.

The Seven Virtues of Bushido

  • 1. Integrity
  • 2. Respect
  • 3. Heroic Courage
  • 4. Honour
  • 5. Compassion
  • 6. Honesty and Sincerity
  • 7. Duty and Loyality
